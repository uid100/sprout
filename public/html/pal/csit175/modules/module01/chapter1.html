General

My thoughts and observations on computer programming textbooks in general are that typically, the first chapter is dedicated to sharing the author's perspective of the history of the particular language or topic, how, when, and why it became its current state, and why it matters.

For experienced developers, this information is often though-provoking and relevant. In many cases, this information will be lost or ignored by beginning developers. Often, I recommend that students browse the contents of the first chapter, read any sections that are familiar or interesting, and gloss over historical or comparative facts. I do feel that in most cases, the chapter one material is worth revisiting after the student has gained some experience with the next few chapters and has enough foundational knowledge to appreciate and understand the author's perspective.

Again, this is a generalization.

Reading Programming Textbooks

My advice for reading all textbooks, and especially programming textbooks is to start with the Table of Contents. The author will have divided the material into manageable pieces and subdivided these into sections and assigned meaningful headers to each. The Table of Contents is machine generated from these Chapter section headers and subheaders. It is therefore, a carefully designed outline of the subject material.

Starting with the Table of Contents, read through the list of topics that you will be learning. Which are familiar already? Which are of particular interest? Which are confusing or completely unfamiliar? This preview of the chapter agenda is helpful and may prepare your mindset to look for particular points.

Prepared with having read the outline, read through the content, always with your computer ready. Enter and try all code examples for yourself. These demonstrations usually build on themselves, and it's worth taking the time to try them yourself to find what works and what doesn't.

Take side-trips. Follow rabbit-trails. Find out how a piece of code really works by changing it. Try modifying it in different ways. Understand not just that you can copy and pasted someone else's example, but why it works by finding out how to break it.

As a final step, and while the material is still fresh, go back to the Table of Contents and read through this outline again. Do each of the topics have a different meaning to you now? Do you remember what you read? Did you miss some content that you want to go back and refresh or clarify?

If you are reading for retention, always start and finish with reviewing this outline.
